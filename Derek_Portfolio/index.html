Native News Staff

Journalism teaches the value of reporting on sensitive communities and cultures, and the issues and topics of relevance to those communities and cultures. I was a photojournalist for the 2017 Native News edition, where I traveled to the Northern Cheyenne Reservation to document issues surrounding diabetic patients through the lens of a man who lost both legs to complications with the disease. My photo was the cover of the issue, and the story itself was the center-fold of the issue. This experience taught me how to navigate reporting on sensitive and complex issues in a population with historic distrust of the news media.

Missoulian Internship

My 4-month stint as a videographer at the Missoulian was one of my greatest opportunities for growth in this line of work. I spent the summer of 2017 covering both high-profile, newsworthy stories with relevance to the whole country, and creative mini-documentaries highlighting the natural beauty of western Montana and the people who live here. I taught myself multiple new styles of shooting and editing, as well as had the opportunity to document stories through still photography. After my time at the Missoulian, I am confident that I can hit the ground running in any style or format of visual presentation.

KPAX-TV

I knew next to nothing about reporting when I got the weekend reporter job at KPAX-TV. I had just finished my first semester of journalism studies at the University of Montana. I had quit my job as a restaurant server and was ready to dive head-first into my path as a visual storyteller. “You are the right person for the job. You are the only person for the job,” I had to keep telling myself so I wouldn’t crumble from the anxiety of starting something totally foreign. I eventually fully convinced myself, as well as those who hired me on at the station, of the truth of that mantra. I covered stories around western Montana for almost a year while pursuing my second bachelor’s degree in journalism.

Student Documentary Unit

Professors selected me through a competitive interview process to direct and narrate the 2017 University of Montana Student Documentary Unit. I, along with a very competent producer, pushed our group of ten journalism students to produce the best product to date of their careers. The film follows a 24-year- old Billings resident who lost his best friend to prescription drug abuse and heroin addiction, as well as a member of the Blackfeet Reservation who became addicted to prescription medication following complications with her gallbladder. The film outlines the potential for abuse that comes with over-prescribing addictive medications, as well as what Montana is doing now to spread awareness on the addictive nature of certain prescription medications.
